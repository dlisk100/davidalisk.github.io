This project began as my NYU Stern MBA research paper through the Glucksman Institute for Research in Securities Markets, advised by Professor Felix Montag.

I wanted to study a question at the intersection of AI infrastructure, economics, and product strategy: are LLM API products becoming commodities, or does meaningful differentiation still structure competition?

The short version: in the OpenRouter-routed market, competition does not look like one flat pool of interchangeable models. Substitution appears much stronger within the open-source/proprietary divide than across it.

## Research Question

LLM APIs are often discussed as if they are moving toward commodity infrastructure: cheaper tokens, many available models, and low switching costs for developers who already route calls through an aggregator.

That intuition is partly right, but it leaves out a practical question: when usage shifts, does it shift across the whole market, or mostly among nearby products that users already treat as comparable?

I focused on OpenRouter because it gives that question a concrete empirical setting. Many competing models are available through one routed API marketplace, prices are visible, and switching friction is lower than in a world where every provider has its own separate integration path.

## Why OpenRouter

OpenRouter is not the entire LLM inference market. It is a routed API marketplace, and the sample should be read that way.

That narrower scope is also what makes it useful. The setting puts open-source and proprietary models beside each other in a shared distribution layer. It makes model availability, routing, prices, and usage visible enough to study market structure rather than only benchmark performance or product announcements.

The raw weekly panel covers 53 models, 11 providers, and 27 weeks from August 2025 through February 2026. Total observed routed usage in the raw panel is about 154.5 trillion tokens.

## What I Built

I assembled a weekly panel of OpenRouter-routed model activity and prices, merged in an LM Arena-style quality proxy, and reconciled model aliases across data sources.

The data work included scraping and normalizing OpenRouter model activity, converting daily observations into a weekly model panel, joining price and quality signals, manually resolving model aliases, and labeling models by provider, open-source/proprietary status, generation status, and product bucket.

The final estimation panel aggregates model-level observations to provider × bucket composite products. That aggregation matters: individual model prices barely moved during the sample, so the demand estimates are not estimates for individual named models. They are estimates for composite products such as a provider's flagship, balanced, or value bucket.

## Main Result

The preferred specification is a nested-logit IV demand model with open-source and proprietary nests. The empirical object is substitution across provider × bucket products inside the OpenRouter-routed market.

The headline result is segmentation: substitution is much stronger within the OSS/proprietary divide than across it. In the preferred model, average within-group cross-elasticity is about 0.126, average across-group cross-elasticity is about 0.008, and the implied within/across substitution ratio is about 14.9.

The same model implies an average own-price elasticity of about -0.78 at the composite-product level. I read the exact elasticity magnitude cautiously, but the structured substitution pattern is the more important result.

The segmentation finding remains stable under nearby outside-good calibrations and a stable-roster robustness check. That gives the result a useful interpretation: in this market, open-source and proprietary products appear to occupy meaningfully different competitive neighborhoods, even when they are distributed through the same routing layer.

## Why It Matters

For AI infrastructure, the result pushes against the simplest commodity story. Lower switching costs and shared routing do not necessarily erase product segmentation.

For product strategy, it suggests that OSS/proprietary status is not just metadata. It may bundle differences in trust, deployment preference, performance expectations, cost structure, or buyer taste that shape substitution even when users can access models through the same marketplace.

For economics, the project is a small example of how AI marketplace data can be used to study competition in a market that changes faster than traditional industry datasets usually capture.

## What the Repo Reproduces

The public GitHub repo focuses on reproducing the final empirical analysis from the released derived panel, rather than rebuilding the entire raw scraping pipeline.

```bash
python analysis/assemble_regression_data.py
python analysis/run_nested_logit_berry.py
```

The normal public reproduction path starts from `data/final_panel_dataset.csv`, rebuilds the provider × bucket estimation panels, and regenerates the nested-logit IV results. The raw OpenRouter ingestion code is included for transparency, but it is not required to reproduce the paper's final tables.

## Limits

Scope matters. This is a study of OpenRouter-routed API usage, not all LLM inference.

The estimates are for provider × bucket composite products, not individual named models. The outside option is calibrated rather than directly observed. Exact elasticity levels should be read with care, especially because the market was evolving quickly during the sample.

The main takeaway is the pattern: within this routed marketplace, substitution appears structured by the OSS/proprietary divide rather than behaving like one undifferentiated pool of API products.
